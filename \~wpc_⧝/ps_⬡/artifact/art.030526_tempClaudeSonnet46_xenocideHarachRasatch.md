Your Bayesian intuition is well-placed. Here's the case:
The Hebrew word ratsach (רָצַח)
Of the several Hebrew words that could mean killing, the Old Testament uses a fairly specific vocabulary:

* harag — general killing, slaying (used for warfare, execution, animal slaughter)
* mût — to cause death, put to death (often judicial)
* nakah — to strike down, smite
* ratsach — the word used in the commandment

The telling detail is that ratsach appears almost exclusively in contexts of unlawful, premeditated, or personally motivated killing — not warfare, not judicial execution, not accidental death. The cities of refuge passages in Numbers 35 are particularly illuminating: they use ratsach to distinguish between intentional killing and accidental killing, and even there the accidental killer is called a rotseach (the noun form) — but the text goes to great lengths to separate this from the "murderer" who deserves death.
The Bayesian case:
If we assign prior probability based on the word's ~47 appearances in the Hebrew Bible, the overwhelming contextual weight falls toward intentional, unlawful killing — what we'd call murder. The contexts where ratsach appears that could stretch toward "kill" broadly are genuinely rare and debated even among Hebrew scholars.
I'd roughly estimate the posterior probability distribution something like:

| Interpretation | Estimated Posterior |
| :----- | :----- |
| Murder (intentional, unlawful) | ~78% |
| Kill (broad, including lawful) | ~14% |
| Genuinely ambiguous / context-dependent | ~8% |

The theological consequence is enormous. If the commandment is "do not kill," it creates an immediate tension with God repeatedly commanding warfare and capital punishment elsewhere in the Torah — making the text internally contradictory. If it is "do not murder," that tension largely dissolves, since warfare and execution carried out under divine mandate or judicial process would fall outside ratsach's semantic range.
That internal coherence argument alone is a strong ontological prior pushing toward "murder" — because a text that commands something and simultaneously prohibits it is either self-defeating or requires increasingly elaborate interpretive gymnastics to rescue.
The NIV, ESV, NASB, and most modern scholarly translations have all moved to "murder" precisely for these reasons. The KJV's "kill" (1611) reflected both the English of its era and a slightly different translational philosophy, and it pulled Catholic tradition along with it for centuries.
So yes — your Bayesian instinct is well-calibrated. "Murder" is almost certainly the more accurate rendering.
